Research Paper Explained

AI That Maps Cracks in Mine Walls — Automatically

Underground mines are riddled with fractures that can cause dangerous collapses. This research built a system that scans walls with a drone laser and automatically identifies every crack pattern — faster and more accurately than any previous method.

Scroll to explore

The Challenge

Why Mine Walls Are Hard to Map

Rock underground is never smooth. It's cut by countless fractures called discontinuities — joints, cracks, and bedding planes. These weak spots control whether a tunnel stays up or collapses.

⛏️

What are discontinuities?

Cracks, fractures, and layering planes in rock. They're mechanically weak zones where failure can start — like perforations in paper.

📐

Why map them?

Knowing which direction cracks run, how steep they are, and how many exist lets engineers design safe tunnels and predict rock falls.

🚁

The scanning part

A DJI drone with a Velodyne laser scanner flies into a "stope" (excavated chamber) and records millions of 3D points — a point cloud of the rock surface.

🤔

The hard part

Automatically turning noisy 3D scans into labelled crack families — especially in fully enclosed underground spaces where every direction is present.

🎯 What a stope looks like (top-down schematic)

A stope is a chamber blasted out during ore extraction — rock faces on all sides, full of differently-angled crack patterns (shown as coloured lines)

The Solution

A 4-Step Pipeline

The researchers built a fully automated workflow. Click each step to learn more.

01

Scan the cave with a drone laser

A LiDAR-equipped drone flies through the mine stope, capturing ~4 million 3D points at ~2.5 cm spacing.

Tool used: Hovermap scanner on a DJI M210 drone — a SLAM-based system that doesn't need GPS because it maps using its own laser returns. The result is a dense, georeferenced 3D point cloud with true north orientation.
02

Single-shot filtering — keep only flat surfaces

A signal-processing trick (FFT on elevation angles) identifies and removes noise, edges, rock bolts, and rough patches in one pass.

How it works: For every point, look at neighbours in a sphere. Measure their elevation angles vs azimuth angles. Flat surfaces produce a clean sinusoidal signal. Rough/noisy points produce a messy signal. Measure the "messiness" (standard deviation of secondary FFT frequencies). If SD > 1, discard the point.
03

Cyclic orientation transformation — fix the compass wrap

Crack orientations (dip angle + dip direction) have a circular nature: 0° and 360° are the same direction. A custom math transformation preserves this.

The bug it fixes: If cracks at 5° and 355° exist (nearly the same direction), standard clustering would place them at opposite ends of the axis and think they're completely different! The cyclic transformation wraps them into a unit circle so nearby angles cluster correctly.
04

Hierarchical clustering — group cracks into families

HDBSCAN automatically groups similar orientations into discontinuity sets, then DBSCAN separates individual planes within each set.

Why not k-means? k-means needs you to say how many groups exist in advance. HDBSCAN figures it out automatically, handles groups of different sizes, and labels outlier points as noise rather than forcing them into a group.

⚠️ Honest Limitations Flagged

The researcher was transparent about three important caveats when sharing results with Mine Excellence:

🧭

Not georeferenced

The Mine Excellence scans lacked global coordinates. So the crack orientations shown are in the scanner's local frame — not true compass north. Georeferencing with survey targets is needed for real-world use.

📉

Weak structural signal

The orientation density graphs showed no sharp, well-separated peaks — meaning these specific scans weren't structurally rich. The algorithm works correctly, but the data itself wasn't ideal to demonstrate full capability.

🕶️

Occlusion / shadowing

Many structural planes were hidden in shadow because the drone path wasn't optimised. A better flight path with wider coverage angles would recover more rock-face data and improve results significantly.

Key takeaway: The algorithm performed correctly on both suitable scans. The challenges were data-quality issues — not algorithmic failures. This is a common reality in deploying research tools in commercial mining environments: the quality of drone flight planning directly controls the quality of results.

So What?

Why This Matters

This isn't just a better algorithm — it's a step toward real-time underground safety assessment. The full mine site had 6 stopes scanned; consistent crack families appeared across all of them, giving engineers a bigger picture of the rock structure.

🦺

Safer mines

Automatically knowing which crack families exist helps engineers design better roof support and blast sequences to prevent collapses.

⏱️

Faster decisions

8 minutes vs 3+ hours. Geotechnical engineers can get structural maps quickly after each blast, not days later.

🤖

No human bias

Manual mapping is subjective. The algorithm finds the same families every time, providing consistent baseline data.

📱

Future: real-time

Next step is GPU acceleration and deployment on drone onboard computers — map cracks as the drone flies, in real time.

The One-Line Summary

"A drone scans a mine with a laser. AI removes the noise, corrects a subtle compass math error, then automatically groups millions of 3D points into labelled crack families — more accurately and 40× faster than before."

The three key innovations: single-shot frequency-domain filtering + cyclic orientation transformation + HDBSCAN hierarchical clustering.
