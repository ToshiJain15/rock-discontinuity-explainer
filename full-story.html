Part 1 · The Problem

Why Mine Walls Are Hard to Map

Rock underground is never smooth. It's cut by countless fractures called discontinuities — joints, cracks, and bedding planes. These weak spots control whether a tunnel stays up or collapses.

⛏️

What are discontinuities?

Cracks, fractures, and layering planes in rock. They're mechanically weak zones where failure can start — like perforations in paper.

📐

Why map them?

Knowing which direction cracks run, how steep they are, and how many exist lets engineers design safe tunnels and predict rock falls.

🚁

The scanning part

A DJI drone with a Velodyne laser scanner flies into a "stope" (excavated chamber) and records millions of 3D points — a point cloud of the rock surface.

🤔

The hard part

Automatically turning noisy 3D scans into labelled crack families — especially underground, where every direction of crack is present at once.

🎯 What a stope looks like (top-down schematic)

A stope is a chamber blasted out during ore extraction — rock faces on all sides, full of differently-angled crack patterns (shown as coloured lines)

Part 2 · The Research (UNSW Paper)

A 4-Step AI Pipeline

Researchers at UNSW Sydney (Dibyayan Patra, Pasindu Ranasinghe, Bikram Banerjee, Simit Raval) built a fully automated workflow. Click each step to learn more.

01

Scan the cave with a drone laser

A LiDAR-equipped drone (Hovermap on a DJI M210) flies through the mine stope, capturing ~4 million 3D points at ~2.5 cm spacing.

Tool used: A SLAM-based mobile laser scanner that doesn't need GPS — it maps using its own laser returns. The result is a dense, georeferenced 3D point cloud, oriented to true north using ground-control survey targets.
02

Single-shot filtering — keep only flat surfaces

A signal-processing trick (FFT on elevation angles) identifies and removes noise, edges, rock bolts, and rough patches in one pass.

How it works: For every point, look at neighbours in a sphere. Measure their elevation angles vs azimuth angles. Flat surfaces produce a clean sinusoidal signal. Rough/noisy points produce a messy signal. Measure the "messiness" (standard deviation of secondary FFT frequencies) — if it's too high, discard the point.
03

Cyclic orientation transformation — fix the compass wrap

Crack orientations (dip angle + dip direction) have a circular nature: 0° and 360° are the same direction. A custom math transformation preserves this.

The bug it fixes: If cracks at 5° and 355° exist (nearly the same direction), standard clustering would place them at opposite ends of the axis and think they're completely different! The cyclic transformation wraps them into a unit circle so nearby angles cluster correctly.
04

Hierarchical clustering — group cracks into families

HDBSCAN automatically groups similar orientations into discontinuity sets, then DBSCAN separates individual planes within each set.

Why not k-means? k-means needs you to say how many groups exist in advance. HDBSCAN figures it out automatically, handles groups of different sizes, and labels outlier points as noise rather than forcing them into a group.

📡 Step 2 — Signal filtering: flat vs bumpy surfaces

Each point in the scan generates a wave signal based on its neighbours' angles. Flat crack surfaces make clean sine waves. Bumpy/noisy regions make messy signals.

✅ Flat surface — clean sine wave → KEPT

❌ Bumpy/noisy surface — messy wave → REMOVED

🧭 Step 3 — The compass wrap problem & fix

Dip direction is circular (like compass bearings). 5° and 355° are nearly the same, but a straight number line shows them 350° apart.

❌ Problem: Normal Cartesian Plot

Points near 0° and 360° are split apart — wrong clusters!

✅ Fix: Cyclic Transformation

Points wrap into a circle — neighbours stay neighbours!

Part 5 · Build vs. License vs. Buy

Three Ways Mine Excellence Could Get This Technology

Once the pitch was made, the obvious next question was: should Mine Excellence license UNSW's exact algorithm, build something similar themselves with free tools, or buy an existing commercial product? An internal technical analysis laid out all three paths.

1

License or Co-Develop with UNSW

Recommended first step

Since the relationship is already established, the fastest route to a defensible, novel product is to formalise a research partnership or licensing deal through UNSW's tech-transfer office — potentially securing exclusive commercial rights for a region (e.g. India / South Africa). The research code would still need "productisation" work: wrapping it in an API, building a UI, and integrating it into the existing point-cloud pipeline.

💡 The strategic recommendation

  1. Formalise the UNSW partnership — seek licensing or co-development rights, especially for specific regional markets.
  2. Define data requirements clearly — both algorithms need annotated, georeferenced, time-series point clouds with geotechnical ground truth.
  3. Position it as a BIMSu extension — a "post-blast structural intelligence" module: scan after each blast, auto-generate discontinuity + bolt condition reports.
  4. Near-term stopgap — use free tools like CloudCompare + DSE for semi-automated mapping now, while deeper integration is developed.

🔩 Use Case 2: AI Rock Bolt Detection & Stability Visualisation

This is a second, related UNSW model called DeepBolt — a deep-learning system that automatically finds rock support bolts in 3D scans, even when they're small and partially hidden.

🤖 How DeepBolt works

Rock bolts are tiny relative to the size of a whole tunnel scan — a classic "needle in a haystack" problem (technically called severe class imbalance). DeepBolt uses a backbone called Cylinder3D, which organises the 3D data into cylindrical slices (matching how a rotating LiDAR scanner naturally sees the world) rather than a standard cube grid. It reportedly beat other state-of-the-art segmentation models by up to 42.5% in detection accuracy, reaching 96.41% precision and 96.96% recall.

⚠️

Why bolt monitoring matters

Ground instability is a major safety risk; manual inspections are slow and reactive, leaving gaps in support verification.

🔄

The workflow

Drone-mounted LiDAR captures the scan → AI recognises bolts → stability map is generated → risks get flagged in a dashboard.

✅

Claimed benefits

Faster inspections, optimised support planning, early hazard detection, fewer rockfall incidents, less wasted material.

🗺️

Holistic view

Combines bolt orientation (red lines) with discontinuity plane sets (coloured surfaces) in one 3D view plus a stereonet summary.

⚠️ Honest Limitations Flagged

The researcher was transparent about three caveats when sharing results:

🧭

Not georeferenced

These scans lacked global coordinates, so crack orientations shown are in the scanner's local frame — not true compass north. Georeferencing with survey targets is needed for real-world use.

📉

Weak structural signal

Orientation density graphs showed no sharp, well-separated peaks — these specific scans weren't structurally rich enough to fully showcase the algorithm.

🕶️

Occlusion / shadowing

Many structural planes were hidden in shadow because the drone flight path wasn't optimised for full coverage.

Key takeaway: The algorithm performed correctly on both suitable scans. The challenges were data-quality issues — not algorithmic failures. This is a common reality in deploying research tools commercially: the quality of drone flight planning directly controls the quality of results. It also tempers the rosier numbers from the business-case deck — real deployments need clean, georeferenced, well-planned scan data to deliver on those projections.

Part 8 · So What?

Putting the Whole Story Together

This case shows the full lifecycle of a piece of mining AI — from a peer-reviewed paper, through a commercial pitch with rosy projections, into the messy reality of an actual mine's data. All three stages are useful to understand, especially if you're the one deciding whether to invest in technology like this.

🔬

The science is solid

The peer-reviewed paper shows real, measured improvements: ~2° orientation error vs. 6–12° for older methods, and 8 minutes vs. up to 3.5 hours of processing time.

📈

The pitch numbers are projections

40% time savings, 20–30% cost savings, 50% safety gains — these are estimates for a business case, not measurements from a live deployment. Worth verifying independently.

🛤️

Three paths to adoption

License from UNSW (fastest, exclusive), build in-house with free tools (4–10 weeks, needs validation), or buy partial commercial tools (none fully match capability).

🪨

Real data has real limits

Only 2 of 14 real scans were usable. Georeferencing, drone flight planning, and avoiding occlusion matter as much as the algorithm itself.

The One-Line Summary

"A university lab built an AI that reads cracks in mine walls more accurately and faster than older methods — then a mining-tech company tried to commercialise it, made big ROI promises, weighed build-vs-buy options, and discovered that real mine scans need much better data collection before those promises can be cashed in."

The technology works. The pitch numbers are aspirational. The real-world test was honest about both.
